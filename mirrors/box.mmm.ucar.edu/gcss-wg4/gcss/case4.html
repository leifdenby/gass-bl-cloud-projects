Case 4: Daytime convective development over land: an idealized
case based on TRMM-LBA observations.

Download this description and data as a text file

Wojciech W. Grabowski, NCAR
(with data provided by Steve Lang, NASA Goddard)

Version 5, March 28, 2003

The purpose of this case is to investigate the development of the daytime convective boundary layer over land and the transition from shallow to deep convection. Large-scale and climate models usually struggle to represent the observed diurnal cycle of convection over land, with deep convection typically developing too early when compared to observations. The actual sounding and the evolution of surface fluxes as the day progresses come from observations on February 23, 1999, during the TRMM-LBA field campaign in Rondonia, Brazil.

The challenging aspect of this case is the wide range of spatial scales involved. The case features the development of a well-mixed cloud-topped boundary layer from the early morning sounding, formation of shallow convection as surface fluxes increase, and transition from shallow to deep convection. This case does not feature any "large-scale" forcing, i.e., no advective tendencies of temperature and moisture are specified as the simulation progresses. We plan to extend this case to cover the late afternoon convection, the transition from daytime to nighttime, and eventually to the entire diurnal cycle.


The main features of model simulations as follows:

- initialize the simulation from a morning sounding (7:30am local time in this case; the sounding is provided in appendix 1);

- apply evolving surface temperature and moisture fluxes (a simple Fortran code to calculate surface fluxes is provided in appendix 2);

- apply evolving radiative cooling (data provided in appendix 3);

- assume free-slip surface boundary conditions (i.e., no surface friction);

- assume computational domain at least 20 km deep, with gravity wave absorber (for CRMs) above 16 km (tropopause height is about 14 km);

- maintain the mean flow as in the initial sounding; use relaxation approach with a time scale of 1 hr, i.e., add a term -(Ua-Ui)/tau to the horizontal momentum equations, Ua is a HORIZONTAL AVERAGE of the model-predicted velocity (u or v) at a given level, Ui is the initial velocity at this level, and tau=3600 sec;

- run the simulation for 6 hrs (i.e., 7:30am to 1:30pm local time)

- for CRMs, use periodic lateral boundary conditions (double periodic in 3D)

- to allow small-scale excitation of convective instabilities in CRMs, apply random perturbations to the surface fluxes with 10% amplitude and random numbers generated every time step and random perturbations to the temperature and moisture, with amplitudes of 0.1K and 0.1 g/kg, applied in the lowest 1 km every 15 minutes.


At this stage, please use model setup you feel is appropriate to investigate this problem. We need a spectrum of models, both SCMs and CRMs, and a range of CRM resolutions, both in horizontal and vertical. For CRMs, my suggestion would be to run at about 1 km horizontal grid increment and a few hundred meters in the vertical (perhaps, using a stretched vertical grid to improve representation of boundary layer processes). I am running a high-resolution simulation that can be used as a benchmark for other models. If you decide to run your CRM in 2D, align your model in the way you feel is "appropriate" for this problem. It would be desirable to have 2D models aligned E-W and N-S in separate test simulations.

I ran my model with this setup using two sets of spatial resolutions and domain sizes, appropriate for shallow convection (SC) and for deep convective (DC), in both 2D and 3D. In the SC setup, I applied a horizontal/vertical resolution of 50/25m using a grid of 200x200 in 2D, and 128x128x161 in 3D. The SC simulation was run for 3 hrs as convection approached the upper lid of my domain (at 4 km in 3D) toward the end of the 3hr period. In the DC case, I used 500/125m resolution in horizontal/vertical and the same number of gridpoints as in the SC case (i.e., 200x200 in 2D and 128x128x161 in 3D). A small writeup with my 3D results (a pdf file) is available on the new WG4 web page http://box.mmm.ucar.edu/gcss-wg4/model/intercomp.html (look for Case 4 results at the bottom). As you can see in the example of my results (cf. Fig 6 in the writeup), my model is not capable to develop well-mixed convective boundary layer using the DC setup. This most likely impacts development of deep convection. Apparently, the TKE model I am using is not up to the task and most likely a non-local boundary layer scheme is needed. This point can be supported using results from a model with such a scheme.

I also wrote a small program that uses this setup in the context of convective parameterization. I applied Emanuel's scheme. I noticed that the results are sensitive to the vertical resolution and to the parameters in the scheme. This was just to convince myself that the setup proposed is of interest to those that use convective parameterizations. It would be interesting to show that convective parameterization indeed produces deep convection to early in this setup (this seems to be the case in my tests using the Emanuel's scheme).

The procedures to submit model results are described in a separate document.


Happy computing!

Appendix 2. Evolution of surface heat fluxes.

The code below (provided by Steve Lang from NASA Goddard) gives a simple fit to the surface latent and sensible heat fluxes for the February 23rd case. Note that you can either assume some method to distribute this flux over the lowest few model levels, or rely on the subgrid-scale model to do this.

c curve fit to observed LBA sensible and latent heat fluxes
c hrl is the local time in hours
c NOTE: simulation starts at 7:30am (t=7.50)
c FLXL is latent heat flux (W/m**2)
c FLXS is sensible heat flux (W/m**2)

api=4.*atan(1.)
cc time is model time in hrs (time=0 at model start)
hrl=7.5 + time
thea=api/2.*(12.75-hrl)/5.25
xfact=cos(thea)
if(xfact.le.0.) xfact=0.
FLXL=554.*xfact**1.3
FLXS=270.*xfact**1.5

provided at 33 height levels, from 42.50m to 22699.48m above the ground. Moreover, time interpolation is required at every model time step to obtain radiative cooling at a given model simulation time. Please assume that the cooling rate provided at the first level (i.e., at 42.5 m) can be used as the rate below this level (i.e., between the ground and 42.5 m).
